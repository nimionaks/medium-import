Onboarding in the Remote World: Making New Hires Feel at Home (Even When They’re Not)

How do you make a new hire feel at home when they’re, well, at home? Onboarding is crucial, and the process your new hires experience can make or break their commitment to your organisation. A report from the Boston Consulting Group reveals that companies with excellent onboarding strategies experience revenue growth that’s 2.5x higher than their peers. In other words, getting onboarding right isn’t just nice-to-have — it’s a game-changer for your bottom line.

How you measure onboarding success may vary, but this guide should help you establish and achieve what you define as success in the virtual onboarding process.

Why Onboarding Matters (Even More) in a Remote World

Let’s begin with the basics. Onboarding is more than just showing someone where the company kitchen is or sharing the Wi-Fi password. It’s about setting the stage for success, building connections, and making sure your new hire doesn’t feel like they’re lost or floating in space.

However, onboarding remotely is quite unique. You don’t have the luxury of bumping into each other in the hallway or having those impromptu 1-on-1 chats. Everything needs to be more intentional; more deliberate. It’s kind of like planning a big Owambe party, only to realize halfway through that everyone’s attending via WhatsApp call — you need to work a little harder to make sure everyone’s having a good time.

The Core Pillars of Effective Remote Onboarding

Communication

This is the lifeblood of any successful onboarding. Without clear and steady communication, your new hire might feel like they’re stumbling around in the dark, trying to figure things out. Set up regular check-ins, provide a clear schedule for their first few weeks, and make sure they know who to go to when they have questions. A smart move? Set up an onboarding taskboard where they can see their to-dos and tick them off day by day. Trello and Clickup are great tools for this. And seriously, don’t just throw them into the Slack channels and expect them to know what to do — guide them through it, please.

Culture Integration

How do you ensure that your new hire understands your company’s values, norms, and unspoken rules when they’re working from a different location? Incorporate virtual team-building activities, encourage social interactions, and share stories that illustrate your culture. One effective way to foster this connection is by using icebreaker questions during onboarding. This approach, highlighted in this Atlassian piece, helps new hires engage with their colleagues and get a feel for the company culture right from the start. It’s not just about the work; it’s also about the people and how they interact.

Tools and Resources

Ensure your new hire has everything they need, from software to logins, before their first day. A well-prepared onboarding kit that includes guides, resources, and contacts can make all the difference. And yes, this might be a little more work upfront, but it will save you from countless, “Where do I find…?” questions later on. Also, set up a buddy system and assign a mentor. The buddy helps with day-to-day questions, while the mentor provides guidance and support. Together, they ensure your new hire feels supported and integrated from day one.

Training and Development

No one likes to feel like they’re walking a tight line. Make sure your new hire knows not only what they need to do but how to do it. Provide clear training sessions, whether live or pre-recorded, and ensure they know where to go if they need further help. Loom is a great tool for pre-recorded guides. And here’s a tip — don’t overload them on day one. Spread out the training sessions, give them time to digest the information, and allow them to ask questions.

The First 90 Days: Setting the Tone

The first 90 days are a super crucial phase of the onboarding process — they set the tone for everything that follows. Here’s how to make sure your new hire is still smiling (virtually) at the end of it:

Pre-resumption: Establish a connection

Onboarding starts well before the first day. It’s about making new hires feel like part of the team from the moment they accept the offer. Take a cue from Reckitt, the global consumer products company. They send a care package to new hires’ homes right after they accept the job. This approach sets the tone for a great onboarding experience, showing new hires that you’re excited to have them on board and eager for them to feel that excitement too.

Week 1: The Warm Welcome

The first week should be all about making your new hire feel like they made the right choice. Welcome emails, team introductions, and a virtual chat or two can go a long way. This is also the time to go over the basics — company history, values, and a high-level overview of their role.

Weeks 2–4: Getting Their Feet Wet

Now it’s time to dive into the details. This is where the real work begins, with more in-depth training sessions and small projects to help them get acclimated. But remember, this is still a learning period — don’t throw them into the deep end just yet.

Months 2–3: Building Confidence

By now, your new hire should be feeling more comfortable in their role. This is the time to start giving them more responsibility and encouraging them to take ownership of their work. Regular check-ins are still important, but they should be more focused on long-term goals and growth opportunities.

Measuring Success: Is Your Onboarding Working?

How do you know if your remote onboarding is hitting the mark? Here are a few ways to measure success:

Feedback

The most obvious but often overlooked method. Ask your new hires how they felt about the onboarding process. What worked? What didn’t? What could be improved? Anonymous surveys can be a great tool here, as they encourage honesty.

Engagement

Are your new hires participating in team meetings? Are they asking questions, contributing ideas, and generally engaging with their work? Engagement is a strong indicator of how well someone has settled into their role.

Retention

This is the big one. Are your new hires sticking around? High turnover can be a sign that something is off in your onboarding process. On the flip side, if you’re seeing strong retention rates, it’s a good sign that your onboarding is doing its job.

In conclusion, remote onboarding is an art and a science. It requires careful planning, intentional communication, and a commitment to making every new hire feel like they’re part of the team, even from a distance. Get it right, and you’ll not only set your new hires up for success but also build a stronger, more cohesive remote team. So, next time you’re welcoming a new team member from afar, remember: a little extra effort goes a long way in making them feel at home — even when they’re not.
